Is this source an NSM?

This could mean that the stars showing the extra enhancement of actinides could be made by the same r-process site as the actinide-deficient case. For example, maybe NSMs could produce both actinide-rich and actinide-poor cases. [NEXT] Well, we have one observation of a NSM, so we can compare our distribution to observations to test if they agree with a NSM.

NSM lightcurve


Lanthanide-poor blue ejecta + Lanthanide-rich red ejecta

Cowperthwaite+ (2017)

In addition to gravitational waves, the NSM event in 2017 released electromagnetic radiation, which means that astronomers all over the world were able to point their telescopes and follow the light over time, generating a lightcurve. To explain the lightcurve from this event, there must have been at least two components of the NSM ejecta: a lanthanide-poor blue component, and a lanthanide-rich red component. So we can do the same thing with our distribution to test if it matches with this observation.

Two ejecta components

So we split our mass distribution for RetII into these blue and red components and find the lanthanide mass fractions and the mass ratio between them. And we get numbers. Remember this is a mass distribution built from observations.

Two ejecta components

Then we compare that to an independent study of the NSM event, and find pretty similar numbers. It's still possible that this ejecta could be from a different r-process site, but these results are surprisingly similar.

Results derived from r-enhanced stars

Further evidence that an NSM produced
the material in r-enhanced stars like Ret II

What we derived from observations of stars is similar to results derived from a NSM observation. This could suggest that the r-process enhanced stars like Ret II were indeed enriched by a NSM event.
