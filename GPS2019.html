Elements in the Universe

from data in Sneden+ (2008)

Here on Earth and in the Solar System, we find a full range of elements from the light elements created in the Big Bang to the actinides, Th and U. The most abundant elements were made through processes like stellar burning, but the majority of the periodic table is made through neutron-capture.

Neutron capture

Specifically there is the, the slow neutron capture process (or s-process), where the timescale to capture neutrons is slower than beta decay. And rapid neutron capture (or r-process), where successive neutron captures occur before the nucleus beta-decays. As a result the s-process takes place very close to stability, and the r-process very far from stability, requiring extremely neutron-rich environments.

What types of astrophysical sites are capable of producing an r-process? There are many possibilities, and recently the gravitational wave event GW170817 confirmed that neutron star mergers are capable of producing lanthanides, and are one r-process site. But we don't know if they are responsible for the majority of r-process material.

r-process enhanced stars

from data in McWilliam+ (1995), Sneden+ (2003)

We know that this r-process must occur somewhere in the Universe because we observe stars that have these r-process patterns. In black is the abundance pattern of a very famous r-process enhanced star, and the purple line is the r-process abundance pattern we observe in our own Solar System. This is not a fit. The black points basically duplicate this pattern, meaning that these elements were created by an r-process event.

Reticulum II


Ji+ (2016)

A. Ji; Dark Energy Survey/Fermilab
Where do we find these stars? We find them here in the Milky Way, and we also see them in other galaxies. This is a sky image of the ultra-faint dwarf galaxy Reticulum II against some foreground stars. There were a few studies that took spectra of nine stars--these ones circled in red--in Reticulum II. When they measured the elemental abundances of these stars, they found that most of these stars were extremely enriched with r-process elements. You can see their abundance patterns duplicate this r-process abundance pattern. What does this mean? Since this galaxy is so small, it mean that likely a very strong r-process event occurred and enriched this galaxy, so it is likely that all the r-process material in this galaxy was made by one prolific event. Further, these authors argue that a NSM occurred in this galaxy in order to produce all these elements.

Actinide variation among r-process enhanced stars

Holmbeck+ (2019); arXiv:1904.02139

One interesting thing about these enhanced stars is that their r-process patterns are mostly the same (between Ba and Hf). However, there is a distinct different in the heaviest r-process elements, the actinides, Th and U.

These stars were enriched by r-process events, and the question is whether This actinide difference can be interpreted as a hint of a different r-process source. Perhaps the actinide-poor signature was made from a rare type of SN, and the actinide-boost signature from a NSM, for example. Alternatively, the first question we could address is whether an additional site is needed at all.
